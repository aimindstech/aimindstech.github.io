Wikipedia & GDP

Latest GDP statistics from the US and Canada shows almost a V shaped recovery could be still in the cards. With the second wave threatening to spoil the momentum, the course of the economic recovery will depend on our success in containing it.

But let’s take a step back and understand what GDP is. Wikipedia quotes “Gross domestic product (GDP) as a monetary measure of the market value of all the final goods and services produced in a specific time period”.

But what about Wikipedia’s content, for example, which is generated for free by volunteers all around the world. It’s one of the world’s largest and most authentic and consulted references for the majority of the topics, but no one gets paid in this process.

The same is true for countless websites, blogs, forums and other sources of online information where the product is produced and consumed for free. Of course everyone knows Wikipedia and online contents similar to those add enormous value to the economy by empowering people with the required information and ease of access.

Information is no longer costly to produce and reproduce. “Time is money” yet in this modern era so many people are willing to devote their time in producing online content without seeking any money in return. Ironically these values are not accounted for in our GDP measure.

Infact the huge adoption of online information sources like Wikipedia, Google Maps have vastly reduced the need for purchase of hard copies of Encyclopedia, Atlas world maps etc. The purchase of these hard copies would otherwise definitely have added value to the economy and be accounted in GDP.

But since Wikipedia and other online information is zero price, these services are invisible in official statistics. They add value to the economy, but not dollars to GDP - well, directly. So there is a growing need to reassess the way we calculate our GDP measures.
